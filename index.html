When you can't name the feeling, start with your body. What physical sensations do you notice?

MAD Family

Hot face, jaw clenched, fists tight, face flushed, teeth grinding, loud voice

SAD Family

Heavy chest, tearful, tired, slow movement, low energy, throat tight

SCARED Family

Heart racing, sweaty, shaky, nauseous, cold, can't breathe deeply

JOYFUL Family

Light, smiling, warm, energized, relaxed, open posture

POWERFUL Family

Chest out, tall, confident, strong, grounded, clear voice

PEACEFUL Family

Soft breath, still, calm, slow heartbeat, relaxed face

How to Use This Wheel

Start from the outside (physical sensation) and work inward. "My chest is tight" ‚Üí "That's in the Scared family" ‚Üí "I'm anxious." This bypasses the need to immediately name an emotion when your brain won't cooperate. Your body already knows; you just need to translate.

üöÄ Task Initiation Checklist

Breaking through the "Wall of Awful" ‚Äî when you know what to do but can't start

The Sequence

1. Physical State Check

Am I hungry? Thirsty? Need the bathroom? These basic needs hijack executive function. Address them first.

2. Sensory Environment Setup

Noise level okay? Lighting comfortable? Temperature tolerable? Uncomfortable = resistance. Adjust before starting.

3. Body Prep

Put on shoes. Stand up. Do 3 wall pushes. Put on headphones. Change into "work clothes." Physical signals tell the brain it's time.

4. Clear the Visual Field

Clutter is cognitive load. Move 5 things off your desk. Close browser tabs. Clear one space to work in.

5. Gather Materials

Don't start until everything you need is within reach. Getting up to find a pen will break the fragile initiation momentum.

6. The Laughably Small First Step

Not "write essay." Instead: "Open document." Not "clean kitchen." Instead: "Pick up one fork." Momentum builds from motion, not from thinking.

7. Set a Timer (Pomodoro)

Commit to 15 minutes. Just 15. Tell yourself you can stop after. (You probably won't, but the time limit lowers the barrier.)

8. Begin. Badly, If Necessary.

Start wrong. Start ugly. Start incomplete. Perfectionism is the enemy of initiation. Motion first, quality second.

Remember: The Wall of Awful isn't about the task itself. It's about accumulated past failures, shame, and the brain's threat response. This checklist doesn't remove the wall. It gives you a ladder.

üíº Workplace Accommodation Request Builder

A structured template for requesting neurodivergent accommodations under the ADA

Legal Framework: Under the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA), employers must provide "reasonable accommodations" for documented disabilities, including ADHD, Autism, and related conditions. A proper request establishes a "nexus" (clear link) between your disability and the specific accommodation.

Body of Request

You do NOT need to disclose your specific diagnosis. General functional categories are sufficient.

Focus on how it affects your ability to do your job, not medical details.

Name the exact accommodation. Vague requests are easier to deny.

Show how the accommodation directly addresses the limitation.

Citing JAN adds legitimacy and shows you've researched.

They may ask for it, but don't include it in the initial request unless you want to.

Common Neurodivergent Accommodations (From JAN.org)

  • Noise-canceling headphones or private/quiet workspace
  • Flexible scheduling (start/end times) or remote work options
  • Written instructions for tasks (not just verbal)
  • Task lists or checklists provided by supervisor
  • Regular check-ins (but scheduled, not surprise)
  • Modified break schedule (more frequent, shorter breaks)
  • Permission to use fidget tools during meetings
  • Reduction of fluorescent lighting (desk lamp instead)
  • Advance notice for changes in routine or expectations
  • Email communication preferred over phone calls

‚ö†Ô∏è Important Notes

Reasonable ‚â† Perfect: Accommodations must be "reasonable," meaning they don't create "undue hardship" for the employer. A request for "no deadlines ever" would likely be denied. A request for "2 extra days per deadline" is reasonable.

Interactive Process: The employer may counter-offer or suggest alternatives. You don't have to accept them, but refusal must be based on why the alternative wouldn't address your limitation.

Keep Records: Send this request via email or certified mail. Keep a copy. Document all responses. If the request is denied, you may need this for EEOC complaints or legal action.
